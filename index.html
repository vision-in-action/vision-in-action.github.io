Vision in Action

Learning Active Perception from Human Demonstrations

Haoyu Xiong    Xiaomeng Xu    Jimmy Wu    Yifan Hou    Jeannette Bohg    Shuran Song

Paper Video Code Hardware TL;DR

We present ViA, an active perception system for bimanual manipulation.

ViA learns task-relevant active perceptual strategies (e.g., searching, tracking, and focusing) from human demonstrations.

Why do we need Active Perception (a.k.a Robot Neck) ?

Visual occlusion presents a significant challenge in everyday manipulation tasks.

Perceptiopn is inherently active -- we purposefully adjust our viewpoint to capture task-relevant visual information.
In contrast, relying on a static view is often ineffective.

While robot wrist cameras can move with the arms, their motion is primarily dictated by manipulation needs, rather than being driven by perceptual objectives.

The above video shows a failure case of a bimanual setup without a "robot neck" (our baseline). The right wrist camera [R] is obstructed by the upper shelf tier, leading to insufficient visual cues for grasping. The chest camera [C] also fails to capture task-relevant information due to its fixed viewpoint, even when equipped with a fisheye lens.

Why 6-DoF Neck?

Human active perception relies on coordinated movements of both the torso and neck to adjust their head poses. (not just the neck!) On the hardware side, simply mounting a 2-DoF robot neck on a static torso offers only limited flexibility and cannot replicate the full range of human motion.
We employ a simple yet effective solution: using an off-the-shelf robot arm as the robot neck. This 6-DoF neck design allows the robot to mimic whole-upper body motions from humans while avoiding the hardware complexity.

See what the robot sees

Most of today's data collection systems neglect to capture rich human perceptual behaviors, such as searching, tracking, and focusing. This observation mismatch—between what the human sees (egocentric view) and what the robot learns from (e.g. wrist camera view)—hinders the learning of effective policies.

Data Collection

VR teleopearion offers an intuitive way to collect data that captures human active perceptual behaviors. However, direct camera teleoperation approaches often introduce motion sickness 😵‍💫🥴🤢, due to motion-to-photon latency (also known as end-to-end latency in VR)—the delay between a user's head movement and the corresponding visual update on the VR display.

While today's consumer VR headsets achieve acceptable latency for applications like games, robot teleoperation introduces an additional challenge— robot control latency (which can come from your controller code, CAN communication, motor latency, etc.): when users move their heads to teleoperate the robot's physical camera, there is a delay between receiving and executing the action command, causing the robot's camera pose to lag behind. The resulting mismatch in viewpoints between the human and robot leads to motion sickness.

Our method -- Async Teleop: Decoupled view rendering & Asynchronous updating.

We decouple the user's view from the robot's view using a point cloud in the world frame, and we render stereo RGB images based on the user's latest head pose (the green loop). This allows the user's viewpoint to update instantly in response to user's head movements (via rendering), without waiting for the robot's camera to physically match the requested viewpoint—a critical factor for reducing motion sickness. Asynchronously, (the red loop) we update the robot's head and arm pose using user's head pose and teaching arms' joint positions; and update the point cloud using the robot's new observations.

Policy Evaluation

We train a Diffusion Policy that predicts bimanual arm actions for manipulation and neck actions that mimic human active perceptual strategies, conditioned on visual and proprioceptive observations.

ViA uses a single active head camera (uncut rollouts)
Evaluatation results show that ViA (ours) enables the learning of robust visuomotor policies for three complex, multi-stage bimanual manipulation tasks.

Cup Task: The cup is randomly placed on either the upper or lower tier of shelf A. The robot must 1) find and pick up the cup, 2) hand it over to its left hand, and 3) place it on a saucer (randomly placed) hidden beneath shelf B. This task requires active viewpoint switching across different stages to gather task-relevant visual information.
Bag Task: The robot must 1) open a bag, 2) peek inside to locate the target object, and 3) take it out. Success requires both active physical interaction (i.e., opening the bag to reduce occlusion) and active perceptual strategies (i.e., head movement to inspect the bag’s interior).
Lime & Pot Task: The lime may appear on the shelf or on either side of the table. The robot must 1) first find the lime, and decide which arm to use for grasping, and place the lime into a pot, 2) lift the pot using both arms, and 3) align it onto a trivet, guided by the active head camera to ensure precise placement.

What if we remove the robot neck?
We compare ViA with [Chest & Wrist Cameras] baseline (omitting the neck).

Findings: The chest and wrist cameras fail to provide sufficient task-relevant information. As shown in the figure below, the right wrist camera is completely occluded by the upper shelf tier during cup-grasping, while the fixed chest camera lacks visibility of the target objects altogether. In contrast, as shown in the videos, our active head camera dynamically adjusts its viewpoint, allowing the robot to gather more informative visual input and improve task performance.

Do reductant wrist cameras help?
Compared to [ViA], [Active Head & Wrist Cameras] setup includes additional wrist views as visual input. Although the teleoperator does not directly use these views, this comparison evaluates whether they provide additional useful information for policy learning.

Findings: [ViA] consistently outperforms both alternative camera setups across all three tasks. Surprisingly, augmenting [ViA] with additional wrist camera observations ([Active Head & Wrist Cameras]) does not improve performance.
Several hypothesized reasons for this outcome: First, the active head camera alone already provides sufficient information, as the teleoperator relies solely on this view to complete the task. Thus, the visual input from the head camera alone is already task-complete. Second, adding wrist cameras increases input dimensionality without necessarily contributing task-relevant information. Instead, the additional views may introduce noisy observations. In a low-data regime like ours, the added complexity can hinder learning by increasing the risk of overfitting or distracting the model with less informative inputs.

Visual representation matters.

[DP3]: intuitively, we assumed that world-frame point cloud bring a visual representation that is more rebust to moving camera setups. However, we found a common failure mode of the [DP3] is hallucination, where the policy misinterprets the scene and issues incorrect actions. For example, in the cup task, [DP3] often directs the arm to an empty section of the shelf, failing to "find" the actual cup location. We hypothesize that this is due to the the [DP3] representation, which is trained from scratch and lacks pretrained visual priors.

Findings: our method—leveraging a pretrained DINOv2 ViT representation— achieves the highest final-stage success rate across all three tasks. Compared to the two baselines, [ViA] benefits from stronger semantic understanding enabled by the DINOv2 backbone. This allows the policy to actively find the object first before initiating arm actions.
The video below demonstrates [ViA]'s visual understanding in the lime & pot task. [ViA] actively looks around before initiating any arm movements and selects either the left or right arm to grasp the lime based on the object's location.

FAQ

1) Why not just use a 360° camera? While a wider field of view helps robot perception, a 360° camera alone doesn't resolve the problem of visual occlusion. For example, in the shelf task, mounting a fixed 360° camera on the robot head—without a neck—still fails to reveal objects hidden in cluttered scenes. To overcome this, the camera must actively move and adjust its pose to gain better viewpoints.
2) What are the limitations of point cloud rendering? One challenge is sensor noise, even with the relatively good depth maps provided by the iPhone. Second, in our method, single-frame depth results in incomplete 3D scenes. In the future, integrating 3D/4D models, e.g., dynamic Gaussian splatting could significantly improve framework.
3)Why add additional teaching arms (gello)? In our bag task, for example, demonstrators must significantly bend their torso to peek inside the bag, while simultaneously coordinating both arms to retrieve the object. Empirically, we found that holding something in hand helps humans better coordinate their upper-body motions. Alternatively, one can use VisionPro’s hand tracking to control the robot arm via inverse kinematics.
